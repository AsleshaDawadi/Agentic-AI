When the original Apple Silicon MacBook Air debuted, it felt like magic: a fanless, whisper-quiet machine that somehow ran circles around thick, hot Intel laptops. But several generations later, the new MacBook Air M5 proves that Apple has officially entered its "autopilot" era. While the marketing machine will scream about raw synthetic benchmarks and neural processing power, using this machine day-to-day reveals a frustrating truth: Apple is coasting on old laurels while ignoring the foundational flaws of a fanless design.
On paper, the M5 chip boasts impressive single-core and multi-core score leaps. If your daily workflow consists entirely of running Geekbench in a loop, you will be thrilled. In reality, the Air remains trapped by its own chassis. Because it lacks active cooling, the M5 chip behaves like a sports car stuck in rush-hour traffic. Fire up a sustained workload—like exporting a 4K video project, compiling code, or attempting to play a modern game—and the machine hits a thermal wall within ten minutes. To keep from melting, the system aggressively throttles performance, rendering all those flashy chip upgrades completely useless just when you need them most. The bottom of the aluminum wedge gets uncomfortably hot on your lap, serving as a physical reminder of the hardware's limitations.
Even worse is Apple’s stubborn stagnation regarding basic hardware features. This design has remained practically identical for generations. We are still stuck with the same port-starved layout: just two Thunderbolt ports on the left side, meaning a single charging cable and one external accessory will completely max out your physical connectivity without an ugly dongle. Furthermore, the battery life—while still decent—has completely plateaued at the same "up to 18 hours" metric Apple has quoted since 2020. Despite claims of the M5 being more architecturally efficient, none of that efficiency has translated into longer real-world endurance.
Then there is the camera and audio array. The display notch still cuts awkwardly into the menu bar, yet it houses the same recycled web camera that struggles under anything less than studio-perfect lighting. The speaker setup remains unchanged, missing the depth and bass response found on the MacBook Pro line.
Ultimately, the MacBook Air M5 feels less like a step forward and more like an annual corporate obligation. If you are upgrading from an ancient Intel Mac or a base M1, you will notice a speed bump—but you would get a similar lift for far less cash by tracking down a discounted previous-generation model. For everyone else, paying a premium for an M5 badge that immediately throttles itself under pressure makes this generation an incredibly easy pass.
